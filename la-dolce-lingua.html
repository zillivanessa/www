Our approach

Learning with body, voice & real things

La Dolce Lingua draws on three traditions: sensory learning, narrative immersion, and the Montessori principle that children thrive when they're free to explore, touch, and discover at their own pace.

In-person activities

Our favourite games

01

Tombola delle parole

A bingo-style game where children match Italian words to pictures drawn on their card. Each number drawn reveals a new word — listening is the key to winning.

02

Memory tattile

Pairs of cards with different textures — rough, smooth, soft, bumpy — each associated with an Italian adjective. Children feel and say the word, linking language to sensation.

03

Caccia al tesoro lessicale

A word treasure hunt hidden around the room. Children search for objects whose Italian names they've been learning, then bring them back and name them aloud — movement, memory, and language all at once.

Questions from parents

Both work beautifully — it really depends on your child's personality and your family's rhythm. Group lessons at the venue offer peer interaction and the shared energy of learning together, which many children find motivating. Home lessons give the teacher freedom to adapt entirely to your child's space, pace, and interests. Some families try both to see what suits best.

Pricing varies by format, age group, and whether lessons are at the venue or at home. Please get in touch directly for a current schedule of fees — we're happy to discuss what works for your family's situation.

The teacher arrives with a small bag of materials — tactile objects, word cards, a story or two. The living room becomes the classroom. Your child's own toys and household objects join the lesson naturally. There's no rigid structure; the teacher follows your child's curiosity, weaving Italian into play, movement, and storytelling throughout.

For babies and toddlers (0–2), a parent or carer is an essential part of the lesson — we learn together through you. For older children, it depends on the child. Some thrive with a familiar face nearby; others find it easier to concentrate when parents step back. We'll figure out what works best together in the first session.
